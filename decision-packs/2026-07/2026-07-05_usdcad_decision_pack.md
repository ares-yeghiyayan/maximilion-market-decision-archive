## 1. Context

USDCAD is being evaluated in a mixed macro environment: broad U.S. dollar weakness after softer U.S. payrolls is pulling against Canada-specific weakness from trade uncertainty, softer growth, and reduced Bank of Canada tightening expectations.

Reuters reported that U.S. employers added only 57,000 jobs in June versus economist expectations for 110,000. That reduced Fed funds futures pricing for a September hike from 67% to 54% and pushed the dollar index down to 100.83. This is generally a negative impulse for USDCAD if interpreted as broad USD weakness.

Canada-specific evidence is less supportive for CAD. A Reuters FX poll reported that analysts cut Canadian dollar forecasts because uncertainty around USMCA / CUSMA renegotiation is weighing on Canada’s economy and reducing the prospects for Bank of Canada rate hikes. The same report noted Canada sends about 70% of exports to the U.S., that steel, aluminum, autos, and lumber have been hit by U.S. tariffs, and that recent quarterly GDP data pointed toward a technical recession. It also said bearish CAD bets had risen and Canada’s 2-year yield had fallen more than 140 basis points below the U.S. equivalent.

Reuters also reported that the Canadian dollar strengthened to a 10-day high after weak U.S. jobs data and stronger domestic manufacturing data, with USDCAD around 1.4175 and an intraday move to 1.4147. This shows the CAD can benefit from broad USD weakness, but the broader CAD thesis remains constrained by trade and rate-differential pressure.

Oil is an important hidden factor for CAD. Reuters reported that crude prices had returned to pre-war levels while U.S. gasoline prices remained elevated, with structural fuel-market constraints still present. Another Reuters piece described the Iran war as a major energy shock, with crude, LNG, refined fuels, and fertilizer disruptions, while a global-market themes report noted that oil prices had dramatically fallen to pre-war levels and that investors were watching OPEC+ decisions. The oil channel is therefore not clean: CAD may be helped by energy-sector terms of trade when oil is firm, but falling or normalization-driven oil prices can reduce that support.
